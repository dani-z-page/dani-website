### The pattern
- EYEBROW: The pattern
- HEADING: Everyone has a theory. Each theory lives in someone else's department.
- LIST:
  - Marketing is generating more sign-ups, but too few become successful customers.
  - Product is responding to the largest and loudest accounts while broader market needs go unmet.
  - Sales, Product, and Customer Success are working from different definitions of the ideal customer.
  - Customer Success is absorbing friction that should have been solved in acquisition, onboarding, or the product.
  - NRR is under pressure, but leadership cannot agree whether the cause is customer fit, product value, service, pricing, or execution.
  - AI initiatives are multiplying without a clear connection to customer or business outcomes.
- BODY: These are not six separate problems. They are signs that the company is not operating around one coherent understanding of the customer journey.

- BODY: Each view is useful. None is the whole customer.
- BODY: I connect those signals to identify which customers succeed, why others fail to realize value, and what the company should target, build, change, automate, or stop.

### The method — The Growth Operating System
- EYEBROW: The method
- HEADING: A practical operating model for finding—and fixing—the constraints behind growth.
- BODY: The Growth Operating System helps leadership teams move from fragmented functional explanations to one evidence-based view of the customer journey.
- BODY: It is not a departmental audit or a generic playbook. It is a way to identify the small number of constraints limiting activation, conversion, retention, and expansion—and organize the company around fixing them.
- STEP 01 · EXPERIENCE THE JOURNEY: Leadership experiences the journey directly: how customers discover the company, interpret the promise, evaluate the product, reach value, seek help, expand, and decide whether to stay.

- STEP 03 · FIND THE CONSTRAINT: The team identifies the few cross-functional constraints that explain the growth pattern and separates root causes from symptoms.
- STEP 04 · BUILD AND TRANSFER: The diagnosis becomes a sequenced operating plan with clear ownership, measures, decision points, and execution support.

## HOW I WORK

### Hero
- EYEBROW: The Growth Operating System
- HEADLINE: Find the constraint. Align the company. Build the fix.
- SUBHEAD: The work begins with the customer journey, connects evidence across departments, and ends with accountable execution.

### Opening
- HEADING: Most growth problems do not belong to one function.
- BODY: A weak trial conversion rate may begin with an acquisition promise aimed at the wrong customer, continue through onboarding designed for a different level of expertise, and end with a sales handoff that arrives too early—or never arrives at all.
- BODY: An NRR problem may reflect poor customer fit, product complexity, pricing, an undifferentiated service model, or a roadmap shaped around a small number of influential accounts.
- BODY: That is why I do not begin with a departmental audit or a favorite playbook.
- BODY: I begin by building one evidence-based view of the customer journey and identifying the constraint that best explains the pattern.

### 01 · Experience the customer journey
- HEADING: Leadership teams often know the journey through dashboards—not direct experience.
- BODY: We examine the journey from the customer's perspective:
- LIST: What promise brought them in? · What are they trying to accomplish? · What must they understand before they can begin? · Where do they encounter friction? · When do they experience meaningful value? · When would human help change the outcome? · What causes them to expand, stall, or leave?
- BODY: The goal is not performative empathy. It is a shared reference point for the decisions that follow.

### 02 · Connect the evidence
- HEADING: I synthesize the evidence no single department owns.
- LIST: Customer interviews and Jobs-to-be-Done research · Product usage and behavioral data · Firmographic and segment performance · Acquisition and conversion patterns · Sales conversations, wins, losses, and objections · Support interactions and recurring friction · Onboarding, adoption, retention, and expansion · Revenue, cost-to-serve, and customer economics
- BODY: The output is not a larger dashboard. It is a coherent answer to three questions:
- LIST: Which customers succeed? · Why do others fail to realize value? · What should the company do differently because of that evidence?

### 03 · Map the growth constraint
- HEADING: Specific enough to act on. Broad enough to explain the pattern.
- BODY: A useful constraint is specific enough to act on and broad enough to explain the cross-functional pattern. Examples include:
- LIST: Acquisition is attracting customers whose jobs do not match the product's strongest value. · The product requires too much expertise before customers reach a meaningful outcome. · High-potential self-serve customers are not offered human help at the moment it would change conversion. · Large-account feedback is dominating the roadmap and suppressing scalable innovation. · Expensive human service is compensating for problems that should be addressed through product or AI. · Leadership lacks a recurring process for converting customer evidence into priorities.
- BODY: We prioritize the small number of constraints with the greatest effect on growth rather than producing a long list of disconnected ideas.

### 04 · Build and transfer
- HEADING: The diagnosis becomes a sequenced operating plan.
- BODY: When I continue as a fractional leader, I work with the existing team in short execution cycles, inside the existing stack, with one accountable growth outcome and explicit measures.
- BODY: The engagement is designed for transfer from day one.
- BODY: Decisions, experiment logic, operating rhythms, and ownership are documented so the organization can continue running the system without me.

### AI
- EYEBROW: AI
- HEADING: AI is a leverage decision, not a feature checklist.
- BODY: The wrong question is: Where can we use AI?
- BODY: The useful question is: Where can AI improve a measurable customer or business outcome without degrading trust, quality, or strategic learning?
- STEP ACCELERATE HUMANS: Help employees synthesize evidence, identify patterns, and make better decisions.
- STEP GUIDE CUSTOMERS: Help customers understand the product, complete difficult tasks, or reach value faster.
- STEP AUTOMATE OPERATIONS: Remove repetitive work where the process is stable, measurable, and suitable for automation.
- STEP CREATE NEW VALUE: Enable product outcomes or business models that were not previously practical.
- BODY: Each opportunity is evaluated against customer value, business value, readiness, effort, risk, trust, and measurable return.

### What a diagnostic looks like
- HEADING: What a diagnostic looks like.
- PHASE Before kickoff: Read-only access to agreed data and systems · Existing strategy, ICP, research, and board materials · One internal point of contact · Executive and customer-facing interviews scheduled
- PHASE Week 1: Evidence and journey: Executive and functional interviews · Customer journey immersion · Funnel and instrumentation review · Customer, product, revenue, and service synthesis · Initial hypotheses and evidence gaps
- PHASE Week 2: Constraint and decisions: Segment and cohort analysis · Win, loss, churn, friction, and success-pattern synthesis · Growth constraint map · AI opportunity assessment · Executive working session · Prioritized 90-day roadmap with owners and measures
- STATEMENT: You do not need another list of ideas. You need to know which constraint matters most.

## RESULTS

- SUBHEAD: Twenty-plus years across Product, Customer Success, Customer Experience, and Growth in B2B SaaS.

### Case 1
- TITLE: From product complexity to 67% better conversion
- Situation: A PLG SaaS company had weak trial conversion and a long list of proposed fixes.
- What the evidence showed: Customer interviews, activation drop-off data, and support patterns converged on one central issue: non-technical customers struggled to build successfully before they could experience meaningful value.
- What changed: I proposed and drove an AI-guided product experience and rebuilt onboarding around validated customer use cases and intent.
- Results: AI-guided product adoption increased 3× · Trial-to-paid conversion improved by 67%

### Case 2
- TITLE: From a broad customer motion to stronger NRR
- Situation: A SaaS customer organization was operating reactively while market pressure increased retention risk.
- What the evidence showed: The company was applying the same service motion too broadly rather than concentrating high-touch effort on customers with the strongest fit and expansion potential.
- What changed: I rebuilt segmentation, engagement models, and customer plays around the journeys and economics of the highest-potential ICP segments.
- Results: NRR increased 30% in twelve months · Expansion revenue grew 23% YoY · NPS increased 15 points · High-touch and scalable motions were separated

### Case 3
- TITLE: From customer research to a new enterprise product
- Situation: A PLG video platform needed more durable expansion while managing volatility in a large self-serve customer base.
- What the evidence showed: Jobs-to-be-Done research identified an emerging enterprise segment that wanted access to the company's hosting infrastructure without its full user interface.
- What changed: I developed the product direction and demand case for a usage-based enterprise offering sold through multi-year agreements.
- Results: New enterprise product and revenue lane · Larger, more efficient expansion opportunities · 23% YoY expansion revenue growth · NPS of 54

### Case 4
- TITLE: From organizational gap to an approximately 80-person customer function
- Situation: A no-code platform needed a standalone customer organization during its separation from Intuit.
- What changed: I built and scaled onboarding, professional services, partner programs, education, Customer Success, and community.
- Results: Approximately 80-person customer organization · NRR increased 20% · NPS increased 15 points · Community grew 88% YoY

### Case 5
- TITLE: From rising support cost to AI-first service
- Situation: Product complexity and service demand were increasing cost-to-serve.
- What changed: I built an AI-first support model and connected service intelligence back into product recommendations and decision-making.
- Results: 95% deflection · 90% CSAT · Lower cost-to-serve · Support intelligence became an input to the product's AI layer
